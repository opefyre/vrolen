# Production-fitness audit — cross-industry credibility

**Date:** 2026-06-22
**Auditor persona:** Senior Operations Leader / VP of Manufacturing, 25 years across food & beverage, pharma, automotive, electronics, textiles, chemical, steel, plastics, paper, semiconductor, packaging, brewing, dairy, cosmetics, contract manufacturing, and additive.
**Codebase audited:** `/Users/abosh/Desktop/Personal/Projects/sc-simulator/vrolen`
**Scope:** Domain fit only — can this simulator credibly model the lines I have run? No code was changed. Findings from `AUDIT-ENGINE-MATH.md` and `AUDIT-DATA-FLOW.md` are taken as constraints, not re-litigated.

---

## 1. Executive summary

The simulator's sweet spot is narrow and clear: **discrete, single-source / single-sink, push-flow lines whose stations have a measurable per-part cycle time, where the only material modality is "parts" and where the only stochastic effects worth modelling are cycle-time spread, MTBF/MTTR breakdowns, and per-station defect rates routed to scrap or to a back-of-chain rework station.** Inside that envelope it is genuinely good — Little's-Law-clean throughput math, deterministic seeds, a real OEE decomposition, configurable changeover matrices, weighted product mixes with optional FIFO production plans, and a finite-rate Poisson source. The audit-math report (30/30 pass) confirms the math is honest where it claims to be.

Outside that envelope the picture deteriorates fast. The model has no concept of **mass, volume, batch, or residence time**; no concept of **continuous flow** (chemical conversion, paper, rolling, distillation); no **multi-output station** (one mould cycle that makes 32 parts); no **assembly BOM** that consumes from multiple upstream feeders; no **finished-goods Kanban / pull** semantics; no **tool-wear or cycle-count-based preventive maintenance**; no **routing variation by SKU**; no **temperature, energy, sustainability, or quality-grade** dimension. Workers exist but skill tiers, learning curves, and operator-paced bundle work are absent.

**Top three breadth gaps** (industries we cannot credibly enter): continuous-flow process industries (chemicals, refining, paper, steel rolling); batch process industries dominated by hold time (brewing, fermentation, pharma APIs, dairy); multi-output / kitting / BOM-driven discrete (injection moulding, electronics SMT with feeders, automotive assembly).

**Top three depth gaps** (within the assembly / packaging sweet-spot): no unit-of-measure other than parts; no cleaning / CIP / changeover-by-allergen forced cycles; no scheduled-by-cycles-completed preventive maintenance and no tool-wear.

**Adoption verdict per segment:**

| Segment                                      | Verdict                                       |
| -------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Light assembly / sub-assembly                | Ready                                         |
| Packaging (bottling, cartoning, palletising) | Workable with caveats                         |
| Pharma — solid-dose discrete                 | Workable                                      |
| Automotive — final assembly / trim           | Workable for takt analysis, not for paint/BIW |
| Electronics — PCB/SMT                        | Not yet (feeder runout, reflow as continuous) |
| Continuous chemistry / refining              | Don't pursue                                  |
| Brewery / fermentation                       | Not yet                                       |
| Injection moulding                           | Not yet (multi-output)                        |
| Steel hot rolling / metals                   | Don't pursue                                  |
| Semiconductor fab                            | Don't pursue                                  |

## 2. Sweet-spot scorecard — what the app does well today

- **Honest throughput math on push chains.** Little's Law holds. Bottleneck identification is correct. Line-OEE is derived from the slowest-station ideal-cycle. The 30-case audit-math report is the receipt.
- **Real OEE decomposition.** Availability × Performance × Quality per station, with Setup, Down, Maintenance, Starved, Blocked, Idle separately tracked. Few free-tier tools do this credibly.
- **Deterministic + replicable.** Seedable PRNG, multi-replication runs (1–50) with 95% CIs. Defensible to a sceptical operator.
- **Changeover matrix with product-pair semantics.** Per-station `changeoverMatrix[prev][next]` is in the engine — better than most. (UI exposure is incomplete; see Data-flow audit finding 5.)
- **Stochastic distributions where they matter.** Cycle time, setup, MTBF, MTTR, inter-arrival are all Distribution-typed, not just constants.
- **DAG topology — fan-out, fan-in, merges, branches** — supported by the engine; topology cycle detection is correct.
- **Weighted product mix OR FIFO production plan** at source. Real planning behaviour, not just a single SKU.
- **Per-station defect rate + bounded rework loops** with a per-station pass-limit cap so pathological loops can't form.
- **Finite-rate Poisson source.** Arrivals are not assumed infinite — a real constraint that most demo tools ignore.
- **Materials pool with one-shot + recurring replenishment** including `maxInventory` cap. The right primitive, even if today it is fixed to a "bottles + caps" recipe.
- **Per-station planned-maintenance windows** with a separate "Maintenance" state in the OEE breakdown.
- **Worker pool with skills, shifts, and breaks.** Skill-matched assignment is in the engine; the validator surfaces skill-coverage gaps.

The above is a credible foundation for assembly + packaging. Calling it a "production-line simulator" is honest for that band.

## 4. Cross-cutting capability gaps

Each gap: why it matters, indicative effort to add (S/M/L/XL), priority for v1 (P0/P1/P2).

1. **Unit-of-measure beyond parts (kg, L, m, m³, doses).** Today every entity in the engine is a part. UoM is the difference between "we model packaging" and "we model production". Effort **L** (touches engine types, KPI math, UI labels, validation). Priority **P1**.
2. **Multi-output station (one cycle → N parts).** Blocks injection moulding, multi-cavity dies, oven batches, blister-pack indexing. Effort **M** (CycleExecutor already has parallelCapacity; this is the orthogonal dimension). Priority **P1**.
3. **Assembly BOM consuming from multiple upstream feeders.** AssemblyStation type exists with `inputPorts` but the chain harness only models single-input-per-station behaviour for materials. Real assembly takes 1×A + 2×B + 1×C and won't fire until all three are present. Effort **L**. Priority **P1**.
4. **Tank-as-buffer with capacity in litres / kg + drain rate.** Blocks bottling, dairy, brewing, anything with a holding vessel. Effort **L** (new station type, new flow primitive). Priority **P1**.
5. **Residence-time / conveyor-oven stations.** Reflow ovens, paint flash dryers, tunnel pasteurisers — many parts in flight at once, fixed residence, scrap if residence < or > spec. Engine has Transport (length / speed) but no part-level residence enforcement or temperature spec. Effort **M**. Priority **P1**.
6. **CIP / cleaning / forced sanitation cycles.** Distinct from planned maintenance (which is a calendar). A clean is triggered by elapsed run-time-since-last-clean, allergen transitions, or product changeover type. Effort **M**. Priority **P1** (food, pharma, cosmetics, dairy all need it).
7. **Tool / die wear — preventive maintenance triggered by cycles-completed.** Today maintenance is calendar-windowed only. Press dies, milling tools, roll changes are by cycle/tonnes. Effort **S** (add a cycle-count trigger alongside calendar windows). Priority **P1**.
8. **Continuous-flow representation (m³/h, no part).** A whole modality. Probably the wrong fight — see §7. Priority **P2** (or never).
9. **Hold time / shelf life on inter-station buffers.** Parts that sit in a buffer > T minutes become scrap or downgrade. Effort **M**. Priority **P2**.
10. **Quality grades (A/B/C/scrap), not binary good/scrap.** Engine has one `defectRate` per station; scrap is a single bucket. Real lines downgrade product. Effort **M** (multi-grade routing on the QC station type). Priority **P2**.
11. **Pull / Kanban / finished-goods cap.** Engine is push-only. Real lean lines pull from downstream. Effort **L** (back-pressure semantics through the scheduler). Priority **P2**.
12. **Routing variation by SKU (different SKUs take different paths).** Today every part traverses every station in the DAG. Effort **L**. Priority **P2**.
13. **Energy, water, CO₂e per cycle as first-class KPIs.** Cost-per-cycle exists; energy/sustainability tagging would be a low-effort additive bet. Effort **S**. Priority **P2**.
14. **Skill tiers / per-worker cycle modifiers / learning curves.** Workers carry skill tags but not speed multipliers. Garment, welding, manual electronics lose realism. Effort **M**. Priority **P2**.
15. **Batch IDs propagated with parts (lot traceability).** Pharma, food. Today `productId` is the only carried label. Effort **S** (add a `batchId` to TrackedPart). Priority **P2**.
16. **Temperature / spec-state on parts and stations.** "Must stay between 60 and 80 °C; below threshold → scrap." Effort **L**. Priority **P2**.
17. **Worker break propagation to station state.** Workers have break windows; data-flow audit finding 8 (referenced) noted some break-end wakeup is fragile. Worth a second look. Effort **S**. Priority **P1**.
18. **Bundle / lot transport between stations (move 30 pieces together, not one).** Engine moves one part at a time across edges. Effort **M**. Priority **P2**.
19. **Re-entrant flow / loops in topology.** Semiconductor recipe routing. Engine rejects cycles by design. Effort **XL**. Priority **P2 or never** — this is a different product.
20. **Spatial packing on a build plate (3D printing).** Two-dimensional bin-packing of parts onto a plate. Effort **L**. Priority **P2**.
21. **Mass-balance / yield-as-% of theoretical reporting.** Pharma + chemistry want "yield = output mass / theoretical mass." Useful even in discrete (just a different framing of scrap rate). Effort **S** (a derived KPI). Priority **P1**.

## 5. Recommended roadmap

### v1 launch blockers (P0) — must ship to credibly call this a "production-line simulator"

1. **Inspector authoring for changeoverMatrix.** Engine has it; UI doesn't. Without it, multi-SKU realism is unreachable from the canvas. **Why this segment can't use the tool today:** any packaging or pharma user who needs A→B vs A→C asymmetry has to hand-edit JSON.
2. **Multi-source / multi-sink graphs must run with all per-station settings honored** (currently silently dropped — see Data-flow audit finding 1, partially mitigated). **Why:** Today a fan-in or fan-out graph silently drops capacity, defectRate, setup, changeoverMatrix, cycleByProduct, rework, and skills. The user has no way to know.
3. **Forced cleaning / CIP cycle primitive.** **Why:** without it, no food / beverage / pharma / cosmetics line is honest.
4. **Multi-output station (N parts per cycle).** **Why:** without it, injection moulding, blistering, multi-cavity dies, oven batches are all blocked.
5. **Material-driven station starvation per recipe, not just the single bottles+caps shape.** Engine supports per-station recipes; the UI shoehorns everything into "bottles + caps". **Why:** electronics-feeder runout and any per-station consumable can't be wired from the UI.
6. **Cycle-count-triggered preventive maintenance.** **Why:** any line with tool wear (metalwork, moulding, stamping) cannot be planned without it.

### v2 breadth bets (P1) — unlock new industries

1. **Unit-of-measure beyond parts** (with UoM tagging on materials and parts; KPIs auto-format kg/h, L/h, ea/h). Unlocks dairy, powder food, chemicals-as-batch.
2. **Tank / vessel station with capacity in litres + drain rate + hold time.** Unlocks bottling realism, brewing-as-batch, dairy CIP scheduling.
3. **Residence-time conveyor / tunnel oven station.** Unlocks SMT reflow, paint shop, tunnel pasteuriser, drying ovens.
4. **Assembly-BOM** (1×A + 2×B + 1×C consumed per cycle from named feeders). Unlocks honest assembly modelling.
5. **Skill tiers / per-worker cycle multipliers.** Unlocks garment, welding, manual electronics realism.
6. **Routing variation by SKU.** Unlocks job-shop and any line where different products take different paths.
7. **Quality grades (A/B/C/scrap) instead of binary scrap.** Unlocks food downgrade, textile second-quality, steel B-grade.
8. **Hold time / shelf life on buffers.** Unlocks food, pharma, cosmetics shelf-life modelling.
9. **Pull / Kanban with finished-goods cap.** Unlocks lean-lines and any back-pressured factory.
10. **Energy / water / CO₂e per cycle KPIs.** Unlocks sustainability storytelling — a strong portfolio differentiator with low implementation cost.

### v3 depth bets (P2) — make this best-in-class for the segments we own

1. **Batch / lot IDs propagated with parts.** Pharma + food regulatory narrative.
2. **Yield-as-% of theoretical KPI** (mass in vs mass out, with UoM). Pharma + chemistry KPI dialect.
3. **Bundle transport** (30-piece move between stations, not 1). Garment, packaging-tray realism.
4. **Build-plate batching for 3D printing** (queue + plate + spatial pack approximation). Additive.
5. **Per-station resource contention pools** ("any of 4 cluster-tool chambers" — finite-server queue). Semiconductor-lite.
6. **Temperature / spec-state on parts** with reject thresholds. Steel-rolling-lite, paint-shop-lite, food-cooking realism.
7. **Per-station random-event library** (power dip, water pressure drop, operator absent) beyond MTBF/MTTR. Adds narrative realism.
8. **Sensitivity-sweep over UoM-aware inputs** (cost-per-kg, energy-per-cycle), not just cycle times.

## 6. Things that would be future bets but probably not worth doing

- **Continuous-flow chemistry / refining / distillation.** A different mathematical model (PDEs, PI control loops, mass-and-energy balance). Building it inside a discrete-event simulator turns a focused tool into an unfocused one. Reject this category.
- **Re-entrant semiconductor flows.** The engine architecturally rejects cycles. Adding them is XL and gives a worse semiconductor simulator than Promodel / FabSim. Reject.
- **Real PI-controlled feedback loops** (e.g. a thermostatted reactor). Out of scope for a DES engine.
- **Detailed CAD-style spatial layout / collision** (forklift paths, AGV routing). The spatial layer is enough for worker movement; full AGV routing is a different product.
- **Multi-site / supply-chain network simulation.** The engine is per-line; per-site is the natural ceiling. Network-of-sites is a different fight (and is what AnyLogic owns).
- **3D visualisation.** PixiJS 2D + a clean schematic beats a mediocre 3D scene. Resist 3D until a customer pays for it.
- **Real-time MES integration / live data ingestion.** That is a different product category (and a different sales motion).

## 7. Honest limits — what this simulator will never be a great fit for

State these plainly in marketing and in the docs. A focused tool that says "no" to the wrong customer is more credible than one that promises everything.

- **Continuous-flow process industries** — chemicals, refining, paper, distillation, hot rolling steel. The "part" abstraction is the wrong primitive; energy and conversion balances dominate; PI control is the operating mode. **Don't sell here.**
- **Semiconductor fabrication** — re-entrant flows, cluster-tool chambers, lot-and-wafer dual hierarchy, defect-density yield models. **Don't sell here.**
- **Pharmaceutical API / fermentation / brewing** — tank-as-resource and multi-day holds dominate; the scheduling problem is tank-Gantt, not cycle-per-station. **Not until we ship tanks (v2).**
- **Lines whose bottleneck is the scheduler, not the equipment.** Mixed-model job-shop with SKU-dependent routing — until we ship routing variation, we will give wrong answers. **Not until v2.**
- **Lines where the unit of measure isn't a part.** Dairy, refining, paper, mining, glass float lines. **Not until we ship UoM (v2).**
- **Lines whose realism is dominated by manual-operator behaviour** — skill tier, learning curve, bundle handing. We can approximate but will not be best-in-class. **Pick this fight or don't, but be honest.**

## 8. Closing opinion

The simulator is genuinely strong for **discrete, push, single-source / single-sink assembly and packaging lines with stochastic cycle times, MTBF/MTTR, defects, and rework loops.** Inside that envelope the math is honest, the determinism is real, and the KPIs are the right ones. Ship that band.

The temptation will be to add UoM, tanks, multi-output, and assembly BOMs as "small" additions and call it a process-industry simulator. Resist that framing. The right v2 narrative is "we are the best discrete-line simulator on the market — and now we also do packaging tanks, multi-cavity moulds, and reflow ovens." That is buyable. "We do everything" is not.

The fastest commercial wedge is **packaging + light pharma + automotive sub-assembly + small-job-shop assembly**, in that order. The fastest portfolio wedge is **anything that ships with the changeover-matrix UI, the multi-output station, the cleaning cycle, and the cycle-count preventive maintenance** — those four together demolish 80% of the "I have to translate too much" reviewer feedback this tool will otherwise attract.

Everything else is a roadmap conversation.
